Transforming Fragmented Tooling into Cohesive, Scalable Ecosystems

Principal UX Designer · 2022-Present

Across both Game Production and Core Platform, I led strategic UX initiatives to reduce fragmentation, increase adoption, and create scalable foundations for complex internal systems.

Game Production
- Redesigned the Game Engine Editor update ecosystem to eliminate version mismatch, reduce update anxiety, and increase adoption from under half of users to strong majority adoption — accelerating feature rollout and improving production stability.

Coe Platform
- Drove UX strategy across workspace management, game environment management workflows, and platform self-service — unifying disconnected tools into coherent, research-informed experiences.

The Strategic Challenge

Fragmentation Across Production & Platform

Both organizations faced variations of the same systemic problem:

1

Tool Fragmentation & Low Adoption

Disjointed systems, inconsistent workflows, and bypassed tools led to version mismatch, manual overhead, and delayed feature adoption.

2

Trust & Predictability Gaps

Users hesitated to update or adopt new features due to fear of disruption, unclear communication, and workflow instability.

3

Operational Inefficiencies

Manual coordination, Slack-based tracking, and dependency on tribal knowledge slowed delivery and increased risk.

While the surface problems differed — editor updates in Game Production, workspace and environment complexity in Core Platform — the core challenge was identical:

Create coherent, scalable ecosystems where updates and workflows are predictable, trusted, and frictionless.

RECENT WORK

1. Streamlining the Game Engine Editor Update Experience

CONTEXT
Internal Game Engine Ecosystem
ROLE
UX Lead / Product Designer
PRODUCT
Game Engine Editor, Game Editor Launcher
TIME PERIOD
2025–2026 (Strategic Initiative)

The internal game engine ecosystem suffered from low adoption and fragmented workflows. Less than half of users were on the latest editor version, meaning critical hotfixes and new features took months to reach production teams.

Despite having a launcher tool, the update experience was broken: users bypassed the official launcher, version mismatch errors were common, updates felt risky and disruptive, and manual version tracking via Slack and Jira was required.

The underlying issue wasn't just tooling — it was trust, predictability, and system coherence.

The mission: Create a Cohesive Content Creation Ecosystem where creators are always just "One click away from their work."

The Core Problems

Fragmentation & Low Adoption

~55% of users bypassed the launcher and downloaded builds directly. This created version fragmentation and delayed adoption of fixes.

Update Anxiety

Users feared that updating would "break the build." As a result, they hoarded old versions or delayed updates defensively.

"Updates feel risky and unclear and require manual actions." — User feedback

Manual Overhead

Version tracking relied on Slack and Jira. Incompatible launches caused project de-syncs and merge conflicts.

Strategic Alignment & Approach

As the UX lead, I initiated the discovery phase by conducting qualitative research with developers, artists, and technical artists. I synthesized the findings and shared insights broadly to build a shared understanding of why this initiative mattered — not just what needed to be fixed.

I facilitated a series of cross-functional workshops to prioritize the most critical problem to solve first, define clear problem statements, align UX, engineering, and product around shared objectives, and identify technical enablers required for sustainable change.

We defined a guiding vision: "One click away from my work."

Unified & Seamless
Updates should extend workflows without breaking them
Effortless
Remove cognitive load by automating version management
Autonomy
Allow teams to control major changes without disrupting production

Design Solution: Smart Project Launch System

After identifying the highest-impact problem, I led ideation sessions to generate solution concepts. We explored and refined ideas using AI-generated prototypes to quickly visualize flows and test assumptions, then prioritized features and defined a focused MVP.

1. Smart Project Launch

  • Double-clicking a project file automatically launches the correct editor version
  • Version mismatch errors are eliminated
  • Manual version management is removed

2. Structured Update Journey

Mapped the update experience into: Awareness → Decision → Locate → Action → Launch

  • Awareness: Clear "What's New" messaging with severity labels (Hotfix, Minor Update, Major Update)
  • Decision Support: "Team-Tested" badges, risk labels, and severity indicators to reduce anxiety
View Prototype

3. Non-Blocking Updates

  • Updates download silently in the background
  • Users can continue working
  • Installation no longer blocks productivity

4. Continuity & Trust

Workspace layouts, panels, and shortcuts persist across updates. Preserving context was essential to rebuilding trust in the update system.

Validation & MVP Refinement

Once the MVP direction was defined, I built an interactive prototype to get feedback from the team and users. Feedback was incorporated iteratively, refining both feature scope and messaging before implementation.

AI-Augmented Design Process

This project was one of my first to deeply integrate AI tools into the design workflow. Rather than spending weeks on static mockups, I used AI-assisted coding tools (Claude Code, Cursor) to generate functional interactive prototypes in hours.

This changed the dynamic of stakeholder conversations:

  • Instead of debating wireframes, teams could interact with a working prototype and give feedback on real behavior
  • Engineering could assess feasibility earlier because the prototype demonstrated actual interaction patterns
  • We iterated through 4 major prototype versions in the time it would have taken to produce 1 traditional Figma prototype

The lesson: AI doesn't replace design judgment, but it compresses the time between hypothesis and validation, letting teams focus on the hard decisions about what to build and why.

Key Design Decisions & Trade-offs

We chose silent background downloads over forced mandatory updates.

Research showed that forced updates were the primary driver of launcher bypass. Users needed to feel in control, even if the update was important. The trade-off was slower adoption of critical hotfixes, which we mitigated with severity labels and "Team-Tested" badges that created social proof.

We rejected a single-version policy in favor of managed version diversity.

Engineering initially proposed forcing all users to a single version. User research revealed that game teams at different production stages had legitimate reasons for version differences. Instead, we designed "project-pinned versions" that preserved flexibility while eliminating accidental mismatch.

We prioritized workspace continuity over feature discoverability.

Users cared more about "will my setup survive the update?" than "what's new in this update?" We deprioritized a prominent "What's New" modal in favor of a subtle notification system, preserving the user's workspace state as the primary post-update experience.

Key Takeaway

This initiative demonstrates how UX leadership can reframe fragmented tooling as a systemic workflow issue, align teams around a shared understanding of risk and trust, drive both architectural and experiential change, and transform manual, anxiety-driven processes into predictable, scalable systems. By grounding the work in research, aligning stakeholders early, and shaping the MVP through iterative validation, we turned version management from a source of friction into a strategic advantage.

Scalable by design: The update flow patterns — version pinning, trust signals, and workspace-preservation logic — were adopted across 4 other internal tools without redesign. Solving the right systemic problem creates reusable solutions at scale.
